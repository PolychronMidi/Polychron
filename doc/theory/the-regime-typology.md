# The Regime Typology

## A diagnostic vocabulary for moments of any complex system



## I. The problem of not having words

Most of what goes wrong in teams, in relationships, in one's own life, goes wrong not because of the external event but because the people involved do not have names for the state they are actually in. They think they are in one state when they are in another. They respond appropriately to the state they think they are in, which is exactly the wrong response for the state they are actually in. The external event was survivable; the response to the misdiagnosed state was not.

This is a problem of vocabulary, and it is unusually tractable. Most people operate with a two-state or three-state vocabulary for their own moments — *fine* and *not fine*, or *good / neutral / bad*, or some variant. The vocabulary forces every state into one of the available bins. States that should be distinguished get collapsed together. Responses that should be differentiated get collapsed with them. The result is a consistent pattern of misdiagnosis that produces chronically wrong responses at every scale, from a single person's handling of a single afternoon to a large organization's handling of a decade.

Polychron needs more than two or three states because its music engine has to behave differently in different states. A system steering tension and release across the six dimensions of density, tension, flicker, entropy, trust, and phase cannot get away with "things are going well" versus "things are going badly." It needs finer distinctions that map to real dynamical regimes in that phase space, and it needs the distinctions to be precise enough that the Conductor's response can be tuned to the state. `systemDynamicsProfiler` classifies the system's 6D trajectory into six regimes with 5-beat hysteresis. Each one has a distinct signature. Each one calls for a different response. Each one, it turns out, has direct recognizable analogues in human systems at every scale.

This essay is the field guide. Learn the six names, learn what distinguishes them, learn what each one calls for, and almost every dysfunction you see in yourself and others becomes legible in a way it was not before.

## II. The six regimes

### 1. Coherent

**In the engine.** Stable, well-correlated signals. Density, tension, flicker, entropy, trust, and phase are moving together in consistent patterns. The system is producing music that is internally resonant; each dimension is supporting the others; no signal is pulling against the rest.

**In a person.** The state colloquially called *in the zone*, *flow*, *on my game*. More broadly: the state where one's cognitive, emotional, physical, and attentional channels are aligned. You know what you are doing. You know what you want. Your body, your thought, and your circumstances are consistent with each other. This does not mean nothing is wrong. It means everything that is wrong is being handled by a self that is coordinated enough to handle it.

**In a team.** Everyone is working on the right problem. Communication flows. Disagreements are about specifics, not about what we are doing or why. The team ships.

**In a relationship.** Both partners are present. The underlying agreements hold. Conflict, if it happens, is about this particular thing rather than about the foundation.

**What the architecture does.** Minimal intervention. The Conductor in a coherent regime does not try to push the system anywhere. Dampening is reduced; decorrelation pressure is off; the equilibrator is content. The architecture treats coherence as something to protect, not something to adjust.

**The translation.** When a human system is coherent, *do not intervene*. This is harder than it sounds. There is a strong urge, especially among managers and therapists and parents, to keep adjusting things. In a coherent regime, adjustment is noise. The right move is to observe, enjoy, and protect the coherence from being disturbed by premature action.

**The failure mode.** *Chronic* coherence. No regime should dominate, and coherence especially does not deserve to. A team that is always coherent is not working on hard enough problems. A person who is always coherent has stopped growing. A relationship that is always coherent has stopped encountering each other. Coherence is something to pass through, not to settle into.

### 2. Exploring

**In the engine.** High variance, low coherence. The six signals are moving, but the movements are not correlated. The system is searching for something — a phrase, a texture, a direction — without yet knowing what it is looking for.

**In a person.** The state of not-yet-knowing. Trying different framings, different approaches, different interpretations. Sometimes called curiosity, sometimes confusion, sometimes indecisiveness — the difference between productive exploration and pathological indecisiveness is not the exploration itself but whether it is eventually capable of converging. The state in itself is valuable; premature labeling of it as pathology is what makes it pathological.

**In a team.** The early phase of a project. Nobody quite knows what the problem is yet. Different people are prototyping different approaches. Meetings feel generative rather than converging. The right meeting output is more questions, not decisions.

**In a relationship.** The state of learning each other. Early dating. Also, and healthily, the state of re-learning each other after a change — a move, a new job, a child, a loss. Exploring is what relationships do when the territory has shifted underneath them.

**What the architecture does.** Accepts the variance. Does not try to force premature coherence. The regime distribution equilibrator's squared penalty above 60% exploring kicks in only when exploring *dominates* — chronic exploration is pathologized; acute exploration is welcomed and protected from premature closure.

**The translation.** Exploring should be neither rushed nor indulged. Most of the interventions people make during exploring regimes are counterproductive. Managers forcing premature alignment. Partners demanding clarity before clarity has had time to form. Therapists pushing for insight before the material has settled. Exploring needs *time* — which is the resource specification-heavy cultures are most reluctant to grant.

**The failure mode.** *Chronic* exploring. A person stuck in exploring is chronically unsettled. A team stuck in exploring never ships. A relationship stuck in exploring never deepens into something stable enough to let new kinds of work happen. At some point, exploring has to either converge into coherent or transition into evolving. If neither happens, something is wrong.

### 3. Evolving

**In the engine.** Gradual directional change. The signals are coherent enough to support structure but moving in a consistent direction rather than holding steady. This is musical development — the phrase that builds, the tension that rises, the arc that unfolds.

**In a person.** Growth. Not random change — directional change. You are becoming more of what you were on your way to becoming, or you are learning something that has a shape, or your thinking is moving in a particular direction rather than sampling randomly from the space of possibilities.

**In a team.** The middle of a good project. The direction is established. Progress is visible. Each day's work builds on the last. There is momentum but it is not brittle momentum — changes in response to evidence still happen.

**In a relationship.** The long middle of a good partnership. Both of you are continuing to change, and the relationship is absorbing the change and incorporating it. Not stable — stable would be coherent — but evolving, which is something better than stable.

**What the architecture does.** Supports the direction without over-controlling it. Dampening is moderate. Profile adaptation is active. The system is willing to let evolution continue but watches for the transition to drifting, which looks similar on casual inspection and is the regime that most rewards early intervention.

**The translation.** Evolving is the regime specification-heavy cultures most consistently destroy. The instinct is to lock in the current direction before it has finished evolving — to declare the direction the permanent one, or to name the current state and treat any further change as regression. This kills the evolution. Healthy human systems require evolving regimes to be *protected* from the premature-canonization impulse.

**The failure mode.** *Arrested* evolving — the system stops moving and declares the current state the destination. The arrested state then stiffens into a kind of bad-coherent that no longer responds to new evidence. Alternately, evolving can fail by accelerating past sustainable pace and collapsing into fragmented.

### 4. Drifting

**In the engine.** Slowly losing coherence. The signals are still correlated, but the correlation strength is decreasing. The system is not noticing the loss because each individual beat's change is small. Over many beats, the accumulated drift has moved the system significantly away from where it thought it was.

**In a person.** The state most depression actually is. Not active misery — active misery looks like fragmented or oscillating. Drifting is the slow erosion of what was previously coherent. Motivation that has been declining for months without any dramatic event to blame. Interest that has been draining from things that used to matter. A life that is not on fire but is no longer catching light.

**In a team.** The slow decline of a previously healthy team. Nothing dramatic. Standups continue. Work continues. But the quality is declining, the enthusiasm is declining, the turnover rate is ticking up, and nobody has a single clear explanation because the explanation is accumulated drift rather than any discrete cause.

**In a relationship.** The long, quiet descent of a relationship that is not ending dramatically but is no longer what it was. Each month is only slightly worse than the last. Each individual day could be explained away. Sometimes the partners do not realize it is happening until the accumulated change is undeniable, at which point the drift has usually moved them far enough that recovery is expensive.

**What the architecture does.** Calls it out explicitly. The classification of drifting triggers a different response than coherent — nudging, decorrelation pressure, profile shift. The architecture treats drifting as the regime that most rewards early intervention, because by the time drifting becomes obvious to casual observation, it has usually compounded.

**The translation.** Drifting is the regime most in need of a vocabulary. Most people do not have a word for the state they are in when they are drifting, which is why they do not intervene, which is why the drift continues until it becomes a crisis. Give yourself, your team, your relationship the word. *I think we are drifting* is a specific diagnosis that calls for specific responses, and it is available before the drifting has produced the kinds of signals that are undeniable.

**The failure mode.** Ignored drifting. Drifting can be ignored for a long time. The cost of ignoring it is that eventually it transitions — almost always into fragmented, rarely into a regime that is recoverable without crisis.

### 5. Oscillating

**In the engine.** Periodic instability. The signals are moving but in reinforcing patterns that produce resonance. Something is feeding back into itself, and the feedback is strengthening the signal until the signal dominates the composition.

**In a person.** Neurotic repetition. The same thought, the same argument, the same feeling cycling back through. Rumination. The loop is self-sustaining because each pass through it reinforces the conditions that produce the next pass.

**In a team.** The same meeting happening over and over. The same disagreement re-emerging under slightly different pretenses. The same decision being made, unmade, and remade. Each oscillation confirms to the participants that this is a "real issue" that needs to be resolved, which is what keeps it unresolved.

**In a relationship.** The fight that keeps being had. The same grievance. The same pattern of escalation, retreat, reconciliation, re-escalation. Sometimes called a dynamic. Always self-sustaining.

**What the architecture does.** The correlation shuffler watches for exactly this — pathological correlations including reinforcement spirals and tug-of-war patterns. When detected, graduated perturbations are applied. The architecture does not try to reason with the oscillation; it perturbs it, because oscillations are usually unreachable by reasoning internal to the loop.

**The translation.** Oscillations are usually broken by external perturbation rather than internal reasoning. The reason is structural: the oscillation is the current stable attractor, and any internal move tends to be absorbed back into the loop. What breaks oscillations is something outside the loop — a new input, a new person, a new context, a deliberate disruption. The person, team, or relationship stuck in oscillation usually needs help from outside the system to break the pattern.

**The failure mode.** Ignored oscillation. Like drifting, oscillations are often ignored because each individual pass through the loop does not feel catastrophic. The cost is that oscillations, once established, are extremely stable, and breaking them later is much harder than breaking them early.

### 6. Fragmented

**In the engine.** Multiple signals pulling in different directions. The coherence that might have existed has broken down. Density is pushing up while tension is pushing down; trust is concentrating while phase is dispersing. The system is not producing music at this moment; it is producing noise.

**In a person.** Acute crisis. Not the long quiet of drifting. The visible breakdown. Different parts of the self are making incompatible demands simultaneously. Decisions cannot be made because any decision will betray one of the parts. The person often appears to observers to be falling apart, because the person is.

**In a team.** Open dysfunction. Different factions pulling in different directions, none able to yield because yielding is perceived as losing. Work is not getting done because most of the energy is going into the conflict between factions.

**In a relationship.** The breakdown phase. Not a fight — a fight is oscillation. Fragmentation is the state where the partners no longer share a workable frame for what is happening. Each one's experience has decoupled from the other's.

**What the architecture does.** Treats fragmentation as the regime requiring the most aggressive response. The system does not try to harmonize at this moment — harmonization would require coherence that is not there. Instead, the architecture applies strong dampening, reduces the number of active signals, and attempts to return the system to a simpler, lower-dimensional state from which coherence might be rebuilt.

**The translation.** Fragmentation is not fixed by trying harder to make it work. It is fixed by reducing the load. A person in fragmentation needs fewer commitments, not more. A team in fragmentation needs fewer initiatives. A relationship in fragmentation needs fewer topics of active negotiation. The instinct to push through is usually exactly wrong. The right move is structural simplification.

**The failure mode.** Pushing through. The specific pathology of pushing through fragmentation is that it confirms the fragmentation — the system, already overloaded, is asked to carry more weight, which guarantees the fragmentation will worsen before it gets any chance to improve.

## III. The five-beat hold

The regimes are classified with five-beat hysteresis, and the hysteresis is not a technical detail. It is what makes the classification useful.

A system that re-classifies its regime on every tick chatters. Every small variation in signal produces a new label. The labels become noise. By the time a human tries to orient to which state they are in, the state has been reclassified six times and the orientation was always going to be stale.

A system that refuses to re-classify misses real transitions. The world has moved on, the signals are telling a new story, and the system is still holding the previous label because it was committed to it. By the time the classification catches up, the transition has been missed, and the response that was appropriate for the old state has been applied to the new state for long enough to do damage.

The five-beat hold is the carefully tuned middle. Long enough that a single beat of variance does not cause a re-classification. Short enough that a sustained change gets recognized. The value of *five* is specific to Polychron's beat rate and the dynamics of its signals. For different systems the right hold period is different — a person's emotional-state classification probably wants a hold period of hours rather than seconds; a team's project-state classification probably wants days; a relationship's probably wants weeks; a culture's probably wants years.

The structural lesson is not the specific number but the principle: classification should resist small perturbations but respond to sustained ones. The common human failure modes at both ends are recognizable. The person who narrativizes their mood every hour (*now I'm happy, now I'm sad, now I'm anxious, now I'm fine*) is running with too little hysteresis and has made their own state illegible. The person who has not updated their self-description in twenty years is running with too much hysteresis and has made themselves illegible in a different way.

The question to ask of any self-classification — your own, a team's, a relationship's — is not *what regime are we in* but *how long have we been in the regime we currently think we are in, and is the classification holding because the system is stable or because the hold period is too long*.

## IV. The equilibrator

Every regime has a failure mode, and the failure mode is always the same shape: chronic dominance by that regime. No single regime, including the good ones, should run indefinitely.

The regime distribution equilibrator tracks a sixty-four-beat rolling histogram of regime occurrences and auto-modulates bias directions with a squared penalty above sixty percent dominance of any single regime. This is a specific architectural commitment. The system is willing to be in any regime for a while, but it will actively push against the persistence of any single regime past the sixty-percent threshold, and the push gets stronger quadratically as the dominance gets worse.

The squared penalty is worth attending to. Linear penalties push modestly against modest dominance and give up against extreme dominance. Squared penalties get modestly worried at the threshold and extremely worried past it, which matches the actual dynamics — a regime at seventy percent is not somewhat worse than one at sixty-five percent; it is significantly worse, because the architectural features that generate new states have progressively less room to operate the more dominant a single regime becomes. The squared penalty is the architecture's way of saying the problem gets nonlinearly worse.

For human systems, this translates to: chronic dominance by *any* state is itself a problem, and the problem gets worse faster than the dominance does. A person who is chronically coherent has lost the capacity to grow. A person who is chronically exploring has lost the capacity to converge. A person who is chronically evolving without ever resting in coherence has no stable platform. A person who is chronically drifting is depressed. A person who is chronically oscillating is neurotic. A person who is chronically fragmented is in crisis.

The lesson is not that you should engineer your life to cycle through all six regimes on a schedule. The lesson is that if you notice any regime dominating your recent life — over a sixty-four-day equivalent, or whatever the appropriate hysteresis is — the dominance is itself a diagnostic signal, independent of whether the dominant regime is one you like. Healthy human systems cycle. So do healthy teams. So do healthy relationships. The cycling is not a failure to settle; the cycling is the evidence that the settling would be the failure.

## V. Using the vocabulary

The vocabulary is a tool only if it is applied with discipline. Three disciplines make the difference.

**Accurate classification.** The first task is to correctly identify the regime you are in. This is harder than it sounds. Most people, most of the time, classify themselves as more coherent than they actually are. Coherent is the regime that requires no action, and the wish to take no action biases the classification toward it. Against that bias, you have to ask: what is the variance pattern, what is the correlation pattern, what direction, if any, is the change moving? An honest answer usually places you in one of the other five regimes more often than you would like.

**Appropriate response.** Each regime calls for a different response. Coherent calls for non-intervention. Exploring calls for protected time and no premature convergence. Evolving calls for support and protection from arrest. Drifting calls for early attention. Oscillating calls for external perturbation. Fragmented calls for load reduction. The wrong response, applied to the right diagnosis, produces the specific failure modes listed in each regime section. The right response, applied to the wrong diagnosis, is worse than no response at all because it confirms the misdiagnosis.

**Regime watching over time.** The most important diagnostic signal is usually not the current regime but the sequence of regimes. A system that goes *exploring → evolving → coherent → evolving → coherent → drifting* is telling a story about what just finished and what is now starting. The same current classification — *coherent*, say — means very different things depending on what preceded it. Coherent after evolving is often earned stability; coherent after drifting is often denial.

Most of what therapy, coaching, and good management do is install the habits of accurate regime recognition in someone who did not previously have them. The techniques vary. The underlying skill is the same. Learn to distinguish the six states. Learn to watch their sequence. Learn which response each one calls for. Apply the response without fighting the diagnosis.

## VI. Coda

The six regimes are not exhaustive. Real dynamical systems occupy positions in a continuous phase space, and the six are categorical bins imposed on that continuity for the sake of actionable classification. Finer vocabularies exist. Clinical psychology has dozens of state descriptors. Organizational theory has its own. The six are the minimum vocabulary that distinguishes states that call for distinguishable responses — the smallest set Polychron could get away with while still generating music that sounded like music.

For most human applications, six is already more than most people have. A team with shared access to *coherent / exploring / evolving / drifting / oscillating / fragmented* has more resolution than almost any team operates with. A relationship with shared access to those six has more than almost any relationship operates with. A person who has installed the vocabulary for their own moments has a diagnostic precision most people never develop.

Polychron did not invent these states. Any sufficiently complex dynamical system goes through them. The project's contribution is not discovery but naming — picking six names that are precise enough to support differentiated responses, installing the classification machinery that recognizes which state the system is currently in, and building the architecture that responds appropriately to each.

The names are available for use outside the architecture. They cost nothing to install. They produce, within weeks of serious use, a kind of diagnostic precision that is rarely available otherwise.

Notice which regime you are in right now. Notice which regime the last important conversation you had was in. Notice which regime your team has been dominated by over the last month. The act of classification, even before the response, changes what is visible. What is visible then changes what is possible.

The vocabulary is the cheapest tool in the kit, and one of the most powerful.
